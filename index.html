Lesson 1: Acting Fundamentals & Finding Your Authentic Self

Introduction to Acting

Acting is more than pretending—it's about finding truth in imaginary circumstances. This lesson introduces you to the foundational principles that all great actors understand.

Core Concepts:

  • Acting is revealing truth through character
  • The importance of observation and empathy
  • Stanislavski's system and "emotional memory"

The Actor's Toolbox:

Your primary tools as an actor are your body, voice, imagination, and emotional life. Strong actors develop all four areas.

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • An Actor Prepares by Constantin Stanislavski (Summary sections)
  • Watch: "What is Method Acting?" by The National Theatre

Exercise: The Mirror

  1. Find a mirror and observe yourself closely
  2. Create three different emotional states (joy, anger, sadness)
  3. Note the subtle changes in your face, posture, and breathing
  4. Journal about how these emotions felt physically in your body

Actionable Takeaway:

Create a "Character Observation Journal" where you'll document interesting behaviors, speech patterns, and physical traits you observe in people throughout the week. Choose one person to study in depth, noting their unique mannerisms, voice qualities, and movement patterns.

Final Assignment:

Submit a one-page "Character Observation Report" on someone you've observed this week. Include:

  • 3 unique physical traits or mannerisms
  • 3 vocal characteristics (pace, pitch, vocabulary choices, etc.)
  • 2 emotional states you witnessed and how they manifested physically
  • 1 paragraph on how you might incorporate these observations into a future character

Lesson 2: Script Analysis & Character Development

Understanding the Text

The script is your roadmap. Learning to analyze it properly unlocks the keys to creating compelling performances.

Breaking Down a Script:

  • Identifying objectives (What does your character want?)
  • Recognizing obstacles (What stands in their way?)
  • Finding tactics (How do they try to get what they want?)
  • Marking beats (When do tactics or emotions change?)

The Given Circumstances:

  • Time period and location
  • Relationships between characters
  • Events preceding the scene

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • A Practical Handbook for the Actor (Selected excerpts)
  • Watch: "How Actors Create Characters" with BAFTA

Script Analysis Worksheet:

  1. Character name: __________________
  2. Primary objective (super-objective): __________________
  3. Scene objective: __________________
  4. Obstacles: __________________
  5. Tactics/actions: __________________
  6. Character relationships: __________________
  7. Important given circumstances: __________________

Actionable Takeaway:

Select a monologue (2-3 minutes) from this free monologue database and complete a full character analysis using the worksheet above. Then record yourself performing the monologue twice—once before your analysis and once after—and note the differences in your performance.

Final Assignment:

Submit a completed Script Analysis Worksheet for your chosen monologue, along with a 1-2 paragraph reflection addressing:

  • How your understanding of the character changed through this analysis
  • How this analysis affected your performance choices
  • One specific discovery about the character that wasn't obvious at first reading

Lesson 3: Memorization Techniques for Actors

The Science of Memory

Memorizing lines is a fundamental actor skill. Understanding how memory works can make this process more efficient and effective.

How Memory Works:

  • Short-term vs. long-term memory
  • The role of repetition and association
  • Physical movement and memory retention

Effective Memorization Techniques:

  1. Chunking Method: Break text into small, manageable sections
  2. Recording & Playback: Record yourself reading the lines, then listen repeatedly
  3. Physical Anchoring: Associate specific movements with lines
  4. The Memory Palace: Visualize locations to "store" different parts of the text
  5. Backward Building: Start with the last line, then add the previous line, and so on

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • Moonwalking with Einstein by Joshua Foer (Memory technique excerpts)
  • Watch: "Memory Techniques for Actors" with Jim Carrey

Memory Experiment:

Choose a short poem or paragraph (10-15 lines). Set a timer for 15 minutes. Use one of the techniques above to memorize it. Test yourself by writing it down from memory.

Actionable Takeaway:

Select a one-page scene or monologue and create a personalized memorization plan using at least three different techniques from this lesson. Document your process, noting which methods work best for you. Set a goal to be completely off-book within three days, recording your progress each day.

Final Assignment:

Submit a "Memorization Method Report" that includes:

  • The text you chose to memorize (attach a copy)
  • The 3 memorization techniques you applied
  • A brief tracker showing your daily progress
  • A short evaluation (200 words max) of which technique worked best for you and why
  • One tip you would offer to another actor struggling with memorization

Lesson 4: Physical Acting & Movement

Your Body as an Instrument

Physical expression is as important as vocal delivery. This lesson explores how to communicate character through posture, gesture, and movement.

Physical Awareness:

  • Body alignment and posture
  • Tension and release
  • Character physicality and habitual gestures

Movement Techniques:

  • Laban Movement Analysis (eight basic efforts)
  • Animal studies and character movement
  • Status expression through physicality

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • The Viewpoints Book by Anne Bogart (Selected principles)
  • Watch: "Physical Theater Exercises" by Frantic Assembly

Physical Character Development:

  1. Choose a character type (elderly person, child, someone with high/low status)
  2. Explore how they might:
    • Sit in a chair and stand up
    • Walk across a room
    • Reach for something on a high shelf
    • React to being startled
  3. Document how the physicality affects your emotional state

Actionable Takeaway:

Create three distinct character walks based on different physical characteristics (e.g., someone with a knee injury, someone extremely confident, someone nervous). Practice these walks for at least 5 minutes each day for a week.

Final Assignment:

Submit a 1-2 minute video titled "Physical Transformation" showing you transitioning between three distinct characters using ONLY physical changes (no dialogue). Include a brief written description (100-150 words) for each character explaining:

  • The physical choices you made and why
  • How these physical choices affected your emotional state
  • One discovery you made while embodying this character physically

Lesson 5: Vocal Techniques for Actors

Finding Your Voice

Your voice is a powerful tool for creating character and conveying emotion. This lesson covers vocal techniques to improve range, clarity, and expressiveness.

Vocal Health:

  • Proper breathing techniques
  • Vocal warm-ups and cool-downs
  • Protecting your voice during intense performances

Voice Development:

  • Articulation and diction exercises
  • Projection without strain
  • Modulating pitch, pace, and tone

Accent and Dialect Work:

  • Introduction to the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)
  • Resources for accent study
  • Common pitfalls in accent work

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • Voice and the Actor by Cicely Berry
  • Watch: "Voice Training: Daily Vocal Warm-Up" with RADA
  • Accent Resource: IDEA (International Dialects of English Archive)

Voice Exploration Exercise:

  1. Record yourself reading a short paragraph in your natural voice
  2. Re-record the same paragraph exploring these variations:
    • Change the pitch (higher/lower)
    • Alter the pace (faster/slower)
    • Shift the placement (nasal, chest voice, head voice)
    • Add a simple accent or speech pattern
  3. Note how each change affects the character and emotion being conveyed

Actionable Takeaway:

Create a personalized 10-minute daily vocal warm-up routine drawing from the exercises in this lesson. Practice it consistently for two weeks.

Final Assignment:

Submit a "Vocal Character Profile" including:

  1. A written description (200 words) of your personal vocal warm-up routine
  2. An audio recording (30-60 seconds) of you performing the same text in two contrasting vocal characterizations
  3. A brief explanation (100 words) of the specific vocal choices you made for each character (pitch, pace, placement, accent, etc.) and how these choices reflect character traits

Lesson 6: Auditioning: Preparation & Execution

The Art of the Audition

Auditioning is a skill in itself. This lesson prepares you for different audition formats and helps you present your best self to casting directors.

Types of Auditions:

  • In-person vs. self-tape auditions
  • Cold readings vs. prepared pieces
  • Callbacks and chemistry reads
  • Musical auditions (if applicable)

Audition Preparation:

  • Research the project, character, and creative team
  • Material selection and preparation
  • The "slate" (introducing yourself)
  • Managing audition anxiety

Self-Tape Essentials:

  • Technical requirements (lighting, sound, framing)
  • Best practices for recording
  • Submitting professional quality videos

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • Audition by Michael Shurtleff (Key chapters)
  • Watch: "Self Tape Audition Tips" by Casting Networks
  • Backstage's Self-Tape Checklist

Mock Audition Preparation:

Choose an age-appropriate monologue that showcases your strengths and complete this prep checklist:

  1. Character analysis (who am I, what do I want?)
  2. Emotional preparation (what state am I in?)
  3. Physical/vocal warm-up plan
  4. Entrance/exit strategy
  5. Potential adjustments if asked to change the performance

Actionable Takeaway:

Create a complete self-tape audition following professional standards.

Final Assignment:

Submit a "Professional Self-Tape Audition" including:

  1. A video recording that includes:
    • A professional slate (name, height, agency if applicable)
    • A 1-2 minute monologue appropriate for your age and type
    • Professional framing, lighting, and sound quality
  2. A written "Audition Preparation Sheet" (one page) explaining:
    • Why you chose this particular monologue
    • Your character analysis (3-4 bullet points)
    • One adjustment you would make if a casting director asked you to perform it differently

Lesson 7: The Business of Acting

Acting as a Profession

Understanding the business side of acting is essential for building a sustainable career. This lesson covers the practical aspects of entering the industry.

Acting Resume & Headshots:

  • Creating an industry-standard resume
  • Headshot requirements and recommendations
  • Building credits strategically

Digital Presence:

  • Actor websites and social media
  • Demo reels and content creation
  • Online casting platforms

Industry Relationships:

  • Finding the right agent or manager
  • Working with casting directors
  • Networking effectively and appropriately

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • Self-Management for Actors by Bonnie Gillespie
  • Watch: "What Casting Directors Look For" - Lisa London
  • Actors Access & Backstage (casting sites)

Resume Workshop:

Create or update your acting resume following industry standards:

  • Name and contact information (or agent contact)
  • Physical characteristics (height, weight, eye color, hair color)
  • Training and education
  • Performance experience (categorized by medium)
  • Special skills

Actionable Takeaway:

Create your professional acting portfolio starter kit.

Final Assignment:

Submit your "Acting Business Package" which includes:

  1. A professionally formatted acting resume (using industry templates)
  2. A one-page "Actor Marketing Plan" outlining:
    • Your target market (types of roles, mediums, etc.)
    • Three specific steps you'll take in the next month to advance your career
    • A budget plan for professional materials (headshots, website, etc.)
    • One local networking opportunity you plan to pursue

Lesson 8: Building Your Acting Career Path

Charting Your Course

The acting industry offers many different paths. This lesson helps you identify your specific goals and create a roadmap for achieving them.

Different Acting Paths:

  • Theater (community, regional, Broadway)
  • Film and television
  • Commercials and voice-over work
  • New media (web series, gaming, motion capture)

Training and Development:

  • Acting classes and coaches
  • Degree programs vs. conservatory training
  • Workshops and masterclasses

Creating Your Own Opportunities:

  • Self-producing content
  • Collaborative projects
  • Building a creative community

Recommended Reading/Viewing:

  • An Actor's Business by Andrew Reilly
  • Watch: "Creating Your Own Work" with Issa Rae
  • No Film School - How to Create Your Own Web Series

Career Planning Exercise:

Create a tiered goal-setting plan:

  1. Short-term goals (next 3-6 months)
  2. Mid-range goals (6-18 months)
  3. Long-term goals (2-5 years)
  4. Dream scenarios (5+ years)

For each goal, identify:

  • Specific actions needed to achieve it
  • Resources required (time, money, connections)
  • Potential obstacles and solutions
  • How you'll measure success

Actionable Takeaway:

Design and initiate a 90-day acting challenge for yourself.

Final Assignment:

Submit a "Personal Acting Career Roadmap" that includes:

  1. A clear statement of your acting goals (short, medium, and long-term)
  2. An assessment of your current strengths and areas for development as an actor
  3. A detailed 30-day plan with specific daily or weekly actions you commit to taking
  4. A list of resources (classes, contacts, websites, books) you plan to utilize
  5. One "stretch goal" that pushes you beyond your comfort zone
  6. A method for tracking your progress and accountability

About This Course

This comprehensive acting curriculum is designed to give aspiring actors the foundational skills they need to pursue a career in the performing arts. Each lesson builds upon the previous one, creating a holistic approach to actor training.

Course Objectives

  • Build a strong technical foundation in acting
  • Develop effective memorization and preparation techniques
  • Master physical and vocal characterization
  • Learn professional audition skills
  • Understand the business side of acting
  • Create a personal career development plan

For Instructors

Assignments can be evaluated based on completion, attention to detail, and application of the principles taught in each lesson. The curriculum is designed to be flexible and can be adapted to different time frames and student experience levels.
